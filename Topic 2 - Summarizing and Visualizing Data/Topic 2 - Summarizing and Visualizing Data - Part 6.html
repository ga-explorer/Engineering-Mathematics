Compute the mean and the median of \(1\), \(2\), \(3\), \(5\), \(7\), \(8\), \(9\). You will find that the mean and the median are the same. Give an intuitive reason why the mean and the median are the same in this case.

Solution:

Mean calculation:

\[ \hat{\mu} = \frac{1 + 2 + 3 + 5 + 7 + 8 + 9}{7} = \frac{35}{7} = 5 \]

Median calculation:

Sorted values: 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 8, 9

Since n=7 (odd), the median is the 4th value: 5

Intuitive reason:

The mean and median are the same because the data is perfectly symmetric around the central value (5).

If we look at the values:

  • 1 and 9 are symmetric around 5 (both are 4 units away)
  • 2 and 8 are symmetric around 5 (both are 3 units away)
  • 3 and 7 are symmetric around 5 (both are 2 units away)
  • 5 is exactly at the center

This symmetry ensures that the balancing point (mean) is exactly at the center value (median).

Exercise 4

Find the standard deviation of the points \(3\), \(4\), \(5\).

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the mean

\[ \hat{\mu} = \frac{3 + 4 + 5}{3} = \frac{12}{3} = 4 \]

Step 2: Calculate the variance (with Bessel correction)

\[ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{(3-4)^2 + (4-4)^2 + (5-4)^2}{3-1} = \frac{1 + 0 + 1}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1 \]

Step 3: Calculate the standard deviation

\[ \hat{\sigma} = \sqrt{1} = 1 \]

Alternative calculation (without Bessel correction):

\[ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{(3-4)^2 + (4-4)^2 + (5-4)^2}{3} = \frac{1 + 0 + 1}{3} = \frac{2}{3} \approx 0.667 \]
\[ \hat{\sigma} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}} \approx 0.816 \]

Note: The Bessel correction (using n-1 in the denominator) is typically used for sample variance to provide an unbiased estimate of the population variance. For small samples like this one (n=3), the correction makes a significant difference.

Exercise 5

Compute the covariance and correlation between the ordered sets \(5\), \(4\), \(3\) and \(2\), \(4\), \(6\).

Solution:

Let X = {5, 4, 3} and Y = {2, 4, 6}

Step 1: Calculate means

\[ \hat{\mu}_X = \frac{5 + 4 + 3}{3} = 4 \] \[ \hat{\mu}_Y = \frac{2 + 4 + 6}{3} = 4 \]

Step 2: Calculate covariance (with Bessel correction)

\[ \hat{\sigma}_{XY} = \frac{(5-4)(2-4) + (4-4)(4-4) + (3-4)(6-4)}{3-1} \] \[ = \frac{(1)(-2) + (0)(0) + (-1)(2)}{2} = \frac{-2 + 0 - 2}{2} = \frac{-4}{2} = -2 \]

Step 3: Calculate standard deviations

\[ \hat{\sigma}_X = \sqrt{\frac{(5-4)^2 + (4-4)^2 + (3-4)^2}{2}} = \sqrt{\frac{1 + 0 + 1}{2}} = \sqrt{1} = 1 \] \[ \hat{\sigma}_Y = \sqrt{\frac{(2-4)^2 + (4-4)^2 + (6-4)^2}{2}} = \sqrt{\frac{4 + 0 + 4}{2}} = \sqrt{4} = 2 \]

Step 4: Calculate correlation

\[ \hat{\rho}_{XY} = \frac{\hat{\sigma}_{XY}}{\hat{\sigma}_X \hat{\sigma}_Y} = \frac{-2}{1 \times 2} = -1 \]

Interpretation: The correlation of -1 indicates a perfect negative linear relationship between X and Y. As X increases, Y decreases in a perfectly linear fashion.

Exercise 6

The pairs \((x_{i},y_{i})\) satisfy \(y_{i}=mx_{i}+b\). Answer the following:

  1. Show that the variance of \(y_{1}\ldots y_{n}\) is \(m^{2}\) times the variance of \(x_{1}\ldots x_{n}\).
  2. How does the covariance of \((x_{i},y_{i})\) relate to the variance of \(x_{1}\ldots x_{n}\)?
  3. Show that the correlation between \(x_{1}\ldots x_{n}\) and \(y_{1}\ldots y_{n}\) is either \(1\) or -\(1\). When is the correlation positive and when is the correlation negative?

Solution (a):

Given: \(y_i = mx_i + b\)

First, find the mean of y:

\[ \hat{\mu}_Y = \frac{\sum y_i}{n} = \frac{\sum (mx_i + b)}{n} = m\frac{\sum x_i}{n} + b = m\hat{\mu}_X + b \]

Now calculate the variance of y:

\[ \hat{\sigma}_Y^2 = \frac{\sum (y_i - \hat{\mu}_Y)^2}{n-1} = \frac{\sum (mx_i + b - (m\hat{\mu}_X + b))^2}{n-1} \] \[ = \frac{\sum (m(x_i - \hat{\mu}_X))^2}{n-1} = \frac{m^2 \sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_X)^2}{n-1} = m^2 \hat{\sigma}_X^2 \]

Therefore, the variance of y is \(m^2\) times the variance of x.

Solution (b):

Calculate the covariance:

\[ \hat{\sigma}_{XY} = \frac{\sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_X)(y_i - \hat{\mu}_Y)}{n-1} = \frac{\sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_X)(m(x_i - \hat{\mu}_X))}{n-1} \] \[ = m \frac{\sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_X)^2}{n-1} = m \hat{\sigma}_X^2 \]

Therefore, the covariance is \(m\) times the variance of x.

Slide 107 - Exercise 6 Solution (Continued)

Exercise 6 Solution (Continued)

Exercise 6 (continued)

Show that the correlation between \(x_{1}\ldots x_{n}\) and \(y_{1}\ldots y_{n}\) is either \(1\) or -\(1\). When is the correlation positive and when is the correlation negative?

Solution (c):

From parts (a) and (b), we have:

\[ \hat{\sigma}_Y^2 = m^2 \hat{\sigma}_X^2 \] \[ \hat{\sigma}_{XY} = m \hat{\sigma}_X^2 \]

Now calculate the correlation:

\[ \hat{\rho}_{XY} = \frac{\hat{\sigma}_{XY}}{\hat{\sigma}_X \hat{\sigma}_Y} = \frac{m \hat{\sigma}_X^2}{\hat{\sigma}_X \cdot |m| \hat{\sigma}_X} = \frac{m \hat{\sigma}_X^2}{|m| \hat{\sigma}_X^2} = \frac{m}{|m|} \]

Since \(\frac{m}{|m|}\) equals:

  • 1 when m > 0
  • -1 when m < 0
  • undefined when m = 0

Therefore, the correlation is either 1 or -1 (unless m = 0, in which case all y values are constant and the correlation is undefined).

Summary:

  • Correlation is +1 when m > 0 (positive slope)
  • Correlation is -1 when m < 0 (negative slope)
  • Correlation is undefined when m = 0 (constant relationship)

Interpretation: When y is a perfect linear function of x (with non-zero slope), the correlation is always exactly ±1, indicating a perfect linear relationship.

Exercise 8

Write a Python program to generate random values uniformly in \((0,1)\) and create a histogram with \(10\) bins. Observe the changes in your histograms when you generate (i) \(20\) points (ii) \(200\) points, and (iii) \(2000\) points. Do the histograms become more or less representative of the underlying data distribution with increasing sample size?

Solution:

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Set random seed for reproducibility
np.random.seed(42)

# Generate different sample sizes
sample_sizes = [20, 200, 2000]

# Create subplots
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 5))

for i, n in enumerate(sample_sizes):
    # Generate n random values from uniform distribution
    data = np.random.uniform(0, 1, n)
    
    # Create histogram with 10 bins
    axes[i].hist(data, bins=10, alpha=0.7, edgecolor='black')
    axes[i].set_title(f'Histogram with {n} points')
    axes[i].set_xlabel('Value')
    axes[i].set_ylabel('Frequency')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Observation:

As the sample size increases from 20 to 2000:

  • With 20 points: The histogram appears irregular and may not clearly show the uniform distribution due to random fluctuations.
  • With 200 points: The histogram becomes smoother and starts to approximate the uniform distribution more closely.
  • With 2000 points: The histogram closely matches the expected uniform distribution, with approximately equal frequencies in each bin.

Conclusion: The histograms become more representative of the underlying uniform distribution as the sample size increases. This demonstrates the law of large numbers in action.

Exercise 12

Consider a 1-dimensional data set, which has been standardized. Show that no more than one-ninth of the resulting values can have absolute magnitude greater than 3.

Solution:

This result follows from Chebyshev's inequality, which states that for any random variable with finite mean μ and finite non-zero variance σ², and for any real number k > 1:

\[ P(|X - \mu| \geq k\sigma) \leq \frac{1}{k^2} \]

For standardized data:

  • The mean μ = 0
  • The standard deviation σ = 1

We're interested in values with absolute magnitude greater than 3, i.e., |X| > 3.

Applying Chebyshev's inequality with k = 3:

\[ P(|X| \geq 3) \leq \frac{1}{3^2} = \frac{1}{9} \]

Therefore, no more than 1/9 (approximately 11.11%) of the standardized values can have absolute magnitude greater than 3.

Note: Chebyshev's inequality is very general and applies to any distribution with finite variance. For specific distributions (like the normal distribution), the actual proportion of values beyond 3 standard deviations is much smaller (about 0.27%).

Practical implication:

When working with standardized data, we can be confident that the vast majority of values (at least 8/9 or about 88.89%) will fall within the range [-3, 3]. Values outside this range can be considered potential outliers.

Exercise 13

Compute the Kendall rank correlation between 2, 1, 3, 4, 5 and 5, 4, 1, 2, 3.

Solution:

Let X = {2, 1, 3, 4, 5} and Y = {5, 4, 1, 2, 3}

Step 1: List all pairs (i,j) where i < j

We have n=5 points, so there are 5(5-1)/2 = 10 pairs:

  • (1,2), (1,3), (1,4), (1,5)
  • (2,3), (2,4), (2,5)
  • (3,4), (3,5)
  • (4,5)

Step 2: Determine concordant and discordant pairs

Pair (Xᵢ - Xⱼ) (Yᵢ - Yⱼ) Product Type
(1,2) 2-1 = 1 5-4 = 1 1 Concordant
(1,3) 2-3 = -1 5-1 = 4 -4 Discordant
(1,4) 2-4 = -2 5-2 = 3 -6 Discordant
(1,5) 2-5 = -3 5-3 = 2 -6 Discordant
(2,3) 1-3 = -2 4-1 = 3 -6 Discordant
(2,4) 1-4 = -3 4-2 = 2 -6 Discordant
(2,5) 1-5 = -4 4-3 = 1 -4 Discordant
(3,4) 3-4 = -1 1-2 = -1 1 Concordant
(3,5) 3-5 = -2 1-3 = -2 4 Concordant
(4,5) 4-5 = -1 2-3 = -1 1 Concordant

Step 3: Count concordant and discordant pairs

  • Concordant pairs: 4
  • Discordant pairs: 6

Step 4: Calculate Kendall rank correlation

\[ \hat{\tau} = \frac{\text{concordant} - \text{discordant}}{\text{total pairs}} = \frac{4 - 6}{10} = \frac{-2}{10} = -0.2 \]

Therefore, the Kendall rank correlation is -0.2.

Exercise 14

We define the modified variance and mean absolute deviation (MAD) of \(x_1, x_2, \ldots x_n\) about centrally located point \(a\) as follows:

\[\sigma^2(a) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - a)^2}{n}\] \[MAD(a) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n |(x_i - a)|}{n}\]

Show that the value of \(\sigma^2(a)\) is minimized when \(a\) is the mean of the points in the data set. Show that \(MAD(a)\) is minimized when \(a\) is the median of the points.

Solution for Variance:

To find the value of \(a\) that minimizes \(\sigma^2(a)\), we can take the derivative with respect to \(a\) and set it to zero:

\[ \frac{d}{da} \sigma^2(a) = \frac{d}{da} \left[ \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - a)^2}{n} \right] = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n 2(x_i - a)(-1) = -\frac{2}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - a) \]

Setting the derivative to zero:

\[ -\frac{2}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - a) = 0 \] \[ \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - a) = 0 \] \[ \sum_{i=1}^n x_i - na = 0 \] \[ a = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i}{n} = \hat{\mu} \]

Therefore, \(\sigma^2(a)\) is minimized when \(a\) is the mean of the data points.

Solution for MAD:

The mean absolute deviation \(MAD(a) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i - a|\) is minimized when \(a\) is the median.

This can be shown by considering that the sum of absolute deviations is minimized at the median. For an odd number of points, this occurs at the middle value. For an even number of points, any value between the two middle values minimizes the sum of absolute deviations.

Intuitive explanation: The mean minimizes squared errors (variance), while the median minimizes absolute errors (MAD). This is why the mean is sensitive to outliers (squaring amplifies large deviations), while the median is robust to outliers.

Exercise 15 Solution

Exercise 15

Let \(x_1 \ldots x_n\) and \(y_1 \ldots y_n\) be sets of samples with means \(\hat{\mu}_X\) and \(\hat{\mu}_Y\), respectively. Let the sample standard deviation of the composite data set be \(\hat{\sigma}\). Show that the sample standard deviations and means satisfy the following relationship:

\[\hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{\hat{\sigma}_X^2}{2} + \frac{\hat{\sigma}_Y^2}{2} + \left(\frac{\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y}{2}\right)^2\]

Assume that \(n\) is large and you can ignore the Bessel correction.

Solution:

Let Z be the composite data set containing all x's and y's. The total number of points is 2n.

The mean of Z is:

\[ \hat{\mu}_Z = \frac{\sum x_i + \sum y_i}{2n} = \frac{n\hat{\mu}_X + n\hat{\mu}_Y}{2n} = \frac{\hat{\mu}_X + \hat{\mu}_Y}{2} \]

The variance of Z (without Bessel correction) is:

\[ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{\sum (z_i - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2}{2n} = \frac{\sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 + \sum (y_i - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2}{2n} \]

Now, let's express each term separately:

\[ \sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 = \sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_X + \hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 \] \[ = \sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_X)^2 + 2(\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Z)\sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_X) + n(\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 \]

Since \(\sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_X) = 0\), we get:

\[ \sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 = \sum (x_i - \hat{\mu}_X)^2 + n(\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 = n\hat{\sigma}_X^2 + n(\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 \]

Similarly:

\[ \sum (y_i - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 = n\hat{\sigma}_Y^2 + n(\hat{\mu}_Y - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 \]

Now substitute back:

\[ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{n\hat{\sigma}_X^2 + n(\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 + n\hat{\sigma}_Y^2 + n(\hat{\mu}_Y - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2}{2n} \] \[ = \frac{\hat{\sigma}_X^2 + \hat{\sigma}_Y^2}{2} + \frac{(\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 + (\hat{\mu}_Y - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2}{2} \]

Now substitute \(\hat{\mu}_Z = \frac{\hat{\mu}_X + \hat{\mu}_Y}{2}\):

\[ \hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Z = \hat{\mu}_X - \frac{\hat{\mu}_X + \hat{\mu}_Y}{2} = \frac{\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y}{2} \] \[ \hat{\mu}_Y - \hat{\mu}_Z = \hat{\mu}_Y - \frac{\hat{\mu}_X + \hat{\mu}_Y}{2} = \frac{\hat{\mu}_Y - \hat{\mu}_X}{2} = -\frac{\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y}{2} \]

Both squared give the same value:

\[ (\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 = (\hat{\mu}_Y - \hat{\mu}_Z)^2 = \left(\frac{\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y}{2}\right)^2 \]

Therefore:

\[ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{\hat{\sigma}_X^2 + \hat{\sigma}_Y^2}{2} + \frac{2\left(\frac{\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y}{2}\right)^2}{2} = \frac{\hat{\sigma}_X^2}{2} + \frac{\hat{\sigma}_Y^2}{2} + \left(\frac{\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y}{2}\right)^2 \]

This completes the proof.

Exercise 16

Let \(x_1 \ldots x_n\) and \(y_1 \ldots y_n\) be sets of samples with means \(\hat{\mu}_X\) and \(\hat{\mu}_Y\), respectively, as in Exercise 15. Assume that \(n\) is large and you can ignore the Bessel correction. Use the result of Exercise 15 to argue that the standard deviation \(\hat{\sigma}\) of the composite data set containing \(x_1 \ldots x_n\) and \(y_1 \ldots y_n\) is at least equal to \(|\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y|/2\). Give an example of a data set where this inequality is satisfied as a strict equality.

Solution:

From Exercise 15, we have:

\[ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{\hat{\sigma}_X^2}{2} + \frac{\hat{\sigma}_Y^2}{2} + \left(\frac{\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y}{2}\right)^2 \]

Since variances are always non-negative (\(\hat{\sigma}_X^2 \geq 0\) and \(\hat{\sigma}_Y^2 \geq 0\)), we have:

\[ \hat{\sigma}^2 \geq \left(\frac{\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y}{2}\right)^2 \]

Taking square roots (and noting that standard deviation is non-negative):

\[ \hat{\sigma} \geq \left|\frac{\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y}{2}\right| = \frac{|\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y|}{2} \]

Therefore, the standard deviation of the composite data set is at least equal to \(|\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y|/2\).

Example where equality holds:

Consider two datasets where:

  • All x values are the same: x₁ = x₂ = ... = xₙ = a
  • All y values are the same: y₁ = y₂ = ... = yₙ = b
  • And a ≠ b

Then:

  • \(\hat{\mu}_X = a\), \(\hat{\mu}_Y = b\)
  • \(\hat{\sigma}_X^2 = 0\), \(\hat{\sigma}_Y^2 = 0\)
  • \(\hat{\mu}_Z = \frac{a + b}{2}\)

The composite dataset has half its values at a and half at b.

The variance of the composite dataset is:

\[ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{n(a - \frac{a+b}{2})^2 + n(b - \frac{a+b}{2})^2}{2n} = \frac{(\frac{a-b}{2})^2 + (\frac{b-a}{2})^2}{2} = \left(\frac{a-b}{2}\right)^2 \]

So the standard deviation is:

\[ \hat{\sigma} = \frac{|a-b|}{2} = \frac{|\hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y|}{2} \]

This shows the equality case.

Exercise 17

Argue why the distance between the sample median and the sample mean of a data set is at most equal to half the absolute distance between the sample means of the first half and second half of the data set. Now use the result of Exercise 16 to show the famous result [17, 30] that the distance between the mean and median is at most equal to the standard deviation. Strictly speaking, this result can be rigorously shown only for probability distributions rather than samples (i.e., populations or data sets of infinite size), and one has to ignore the Bessel correction to make it hold for samples. Also assume for simplicity that the data set has an even number of points.

Solution:

Part 1: Distance between median and mean

For a dataset with an even number of points (n=2m), let:

  • First half: x₁, x₂, ..., xₘ with mean μ₁
  • Second half: xₘ₊₁, xₘ₊₂, ..., x₂ₘ with mean μ₂
  • Overall mean: μ = (μ₁ + μ₂)/2

The median lies between xₘ and xₘ₊₁. The maximum possible distance between the median and the mean occurs when the median is at one extreme of this interval.

Since the mean of the entire dataset is exactly halfway between μ₁ and μ₂, and the median lies somewhere between the first and second halves, the distance between the median and mean is at most half the distance between μ₁ and μ₂:

\[ |\text{median} - \mu| \leq \frac{|\mu_1 - \mu_2|}{2} \]

Part 2: Using Exercise 16 to show |mean - median| ≤ standard deviation

Consider splitting the dataset into two parts:

  • First part: values less than or equal to the median
  • Second part: values greater than or equal to the median

Let μ₁ be the mean of the first part and μ₂ be the mean of the second part.

From Exercise 16, we know that for any two datasets:

\[ \sigma \geq \frac{|\mu_1 - \mu_2|}{2} \]

And from Part 1, we have:

\[ |\mu - \text{median}| \leq \frac{|\mu_1 - \mu_2|}{2} \]

Combining these:

\[ |\mu - \text{median}| \leq \frac{|\mu_1 - \mu_2|}{2} \leq \sigma \]

Therefore, the distance between the mean and median is at most equal to the standard deviation.

Note: This is a famous result in statistics. It shows that the mean and median cannot be too far apart relative to the spread of the data.

Exercise 18

A 1-dimensional data set of \(n\) points \(x_1 \ldots x_n\) is partitioned into \(k\) groups \(\mathcal{G}_1 \ldots \mathcal{G}_k\), so that the \(j\)th group has sample mean \(\hat{\mu}_j\) and has \(n_j = |\mathcal{G}_j|\) points.

  1. Show that \(\sum_{j=1}^k \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} (x_i - \hat{\mu}_j)\hat{\mu}_j = 0\).
  2. Use the result of part (a) to show the following: \[\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 = \sum_{j=1}^k \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} (x_i - \hat{\mu}_j)^2 + \sum_{j=1}^k n_j \hat{\mu}_j^2\]
  3. Use the result of part (b) to show the following for any scalar \(a\): \[\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - a)^2 = \sum_{j=1}^{k}\sum_{x_i\in\mathcal{G}_j}(x_i - \hat{\mu}_j)^2 + \sum_{j=1}^{k}n_j(\hat{\mu}_j - a)^2\]

Solution (a):

For each group j:

\[ \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} (x_i - \hat{\mu}_j)\hat{\mu}_j = \hat{\mu}_j \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} (x_i - \hat{\mu}_j) = \hat{\mu}_j \left( \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} x_i - n_j \hat{\mu}_j \right) \]

But \(\sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} x_i = n_j \hat{\mu}_j\), so:

\[ \hat{\mu}_j (n_j \hat{\mu}_j - n_j \hat{\mu}_j) = \hat{\mu}_j \cdot 0 = 0 \]

Since this holds for each group j, the total sum is also 0:

\[ \sum_{j=1}^k \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} (x_i - \hat{\mu}_j)\hat{\mu}_j = 0 \]

Solution (b):

We start with the identity:

\[ x_i = (x_i - \hat{\mu}_j) + \hat{\mu}_j \]

Squaring both sides:

\[ x_i^2 = (x_i - \hat{\mu}_j)^2 + 2(x_i - \hat{\mu}_j)\hat{\mu}_j + \hat{\mu}_j^2 \]

Summing over all points:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 = \sum_{j=1}^k \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} (x_i - \hat{\mu}_j)^2 + 2\sum_{j=1}^k \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} (x_i - \hat{\mu}_j)\hat{\mu}_j + \sum_{j=1}^k \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} \hat{\mu}_j^2 \]

From part (a), the middle term is 0. The last term becomes \(\sum_{j=1}^k n_j \hat{\mu}_j^2\). Therefore:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 = \sum_{j=1}^k \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{G}_j} (x_i - \hat{\mu}_j)^2 + \sum_{j=1}^k n_j \hat{\mu}_j^2 \]
